For companies whose ethical status is ambiguous (e.g. global orgs founded in Israel but now multinational; companies with adjacent-but-not-primary defence revenue; etc.), **don't recommend pre-emptive withdrawal**. Flag the ambiguity once for visibility, then leave the application as-is unless / until the employer actually responds.

**Why:** Luke's stated rationale (2026-05-28): "no point fretting if I don't hear back from them." Most LinkedIn applications get silent rejections; doing the careful research to draw an ethical line for a company that's never going to engage is wasted effort. The right time to do the research is when an actual recruiter screen materialises and a real decision has to be made.

**How to apply:**

- **Unambiguous cases** (HQ in Israel, primarily Israeli workforce; or named defence contractors like BAE / Lockheed / Raytheon / MBDA / Leidos): treat as Luke's stated exclusion and flag for withdrawal or skip from the funnel.
- **Borderline cases** (Israeli-founded global orgs like Snyk / Wix / Monday.com; or commercial firms with secondary defence contracts; or supplier-of-supplier exposure): flag the ambiguity *once* in conversation, but **don't propose withdrawal**. Wait for an actual response.
- **When the response actually lands**: re-raise the ethical question with the concrete trigger ("Snyk recruiter just emailed — time to figure out where the line is"). Then do the research and decide.

This is a more general pattern than just ethical filtering — it applies to any "speculative future decision" about a low-engagement pipeline entry. Don't burn cycles deciding things until they're actually decidable.
