Observed patterns from a review of fourteen of Luke's previous cover letters (reviewed 2026-05-20). Luke self-described as "not very good at them" — these notes inform future drafts and the /tailor-cover-letter workflow.

## Recurring weaknesses

- **Industrial-scale boilerplate.** Four engineering-manager-level letters share the exact same 75-word opener about "managing teams through various points in their lifecycles". Six letters share an identical 60-word career-break paragraph as paragraph two. By word ~130, nothing role-specific has been said.
- **Career break sits too high.** Verbatim "Last year I took a career break..." paragraph appears as paragraph 2 of most letters. By mid-2026 the break ended 5+ months ago; the lucos_agent / agentic-coding-guardrails work is the real headline of his current state, not the break itself.
- **Adjectives, not achievements.** Letters describe traits ("I'm pragmatic", "I work well with stakeholders") rather than concrete outcomes. Quantified or specific stories almost entirely absent.
- **Weak/missing "why this company" paragraph.** Most letters could be sent to any company with a sentence change. Exceptions are the strongest letters (see below).
- **Opens with "I'm a [type of person]…"** rather than anchoring to the specific role first.
- **No salutation on most**, or just "Hi,". Only one letter addresses the hiring manager by name.
- **Several letters lack any close at all** — particularly among the EM-level boilerplate-heavy ones.

## The civil-service material is gold — and locked

Luke's Civil Service Personal Statement contains his strongest evidence by a wide margin: the technical-standards-rationalisation story, the on-prem-to-SaaS migration coordination, the 13-years-at-FT-handling-elections-budgets-pandemics-and-nation-state-actors line, the Tokyo secondment, the cloud-native architecture progression. **None of it appears in his discretionary covering letters.** Mining this material into the building-block library is the single highest-leverage move for future letters.

## What's working — keep doing

- **Career-break voice** is well-calibrated: measured, not buzzwordy ("AI Native" etc.), tonally on-brand for a cyber-security leader's instincts around emerging-tech risk. Don't re-write — just move it later in the letter.
- **The transport-sector letter's public-transport-hobby paragraph** is excellent. The kind of human signal that lifts a letter above the slush pile when the company has a genuine connection (transport, media, public service).
- **A regulated-pensions letter's honest gap note** (no ISO 27001 / SOC2 but adjacent experience like CyberEssentials and PCI-DSS) is the right move on regulated-industry roles. Pre-empts the question, signals candour.
- **Two of his strongest letters** — one to a public-service broadcaster, one to an open-knowledge non-profit — both work because the "why this company" paragraph is *concrete* and tied to public material the company itself produces (Linked Data heritage in one; a multi-layer enablement chain from platform team → contributors → end users in the other).
- **His prose voice in general.** None of these read AI-generated. Preserve voice when rewriting.

## How to apply

When tailoring any future cover letter:
1. Don't open with the boilerplate "I'm an X who…" template.
2. Lead with the role + one sharp claim. Each letter's opening sentence should differ.
3. Pull at least one concrete evidence story from the library (mined from civil-service statement).
4. Demand a real "why this company" paragraph. If Luke can't write 60 words about why this specific company, that's a signal not to apply.
5. Demote the career break to a brief aside near the end; promote the lucos_agent / current-focus work as the forward-looking headline.
6. Always close with a one-line invitation to talk.
