Stated explicitly 2026-05-28 in the funnel-prioritisation conversation.

## The headline

Luke is genuinely indifferent to job titles — confirmed both by stated preference and by the actual title trajectory on his CV (Architect → Principal Engineer → Director → Interim VP → Director). The five-variant CV approach (Architect / Manager / Security / Tech / Extended) is downstream of this indifference, not a stylistic choice.

The real psychological floor is **autonomy over technical decisions in his domain**. Specifically:

- **Acceptable**: being managed by someone less technical than him, provided they let him make his own technical decisions.
- **Unacceptable**: having technical decisions made for him by someone less experienced than him.

This distinction is sharper than the usual "I want autonomy" framing — Luke is comfortable with non-technical management as long as it's hands-off on the tech.

## How this changes funnel triage

**Autonomy is the primary fit signal**, ahead of title-match, comp-match, or domain-match.

### Strong-autonomy JD language (favourable)

- "Set technical direction" / "own the architecture" / "make architectural decisions"
- "First senior IC" / "founding engineer" / "build the function from scratch"
- "Lead by influence" / "you'll be the technical voice for X"
- "We want someone opinionated who'll push back on us"
- "Reports to CTO / COO / founder" — especially when the manager isn't deep-tech
- "Most senior engineer on the team"

### Autonomy-killer JD language (unfavourable)

- "Implement the technical vision set by..."
- "Work closely with our existing technical leadership to deliver..."
- "Follow our established patterns / frameworks / standards"
- "Contribute to" the architecture (vs. "own" or "shape")
- Architecture Review Board / Architecture Council mentioned as a sign-off body
- JD goes out of its way to name a specific Principal / Distinguished / Chief Architect above the role
- Highly-regimented industries with central technical mandates (some banks, parts of central government)

### Ambiguous (probe in the screen)

- "Staff Engineer on the X team" at a company with multiple Staffs — could be team-Staff or function-Staff
- Director roles reporting to a hands-on-technical VP/CTO — Director title isn't sufficient
- EM roles where you're managing technically — depends on whether team has a Principal above

## How this changes cover-letter drafting

For JDs with strong autonomy signals: lead with autonomy-relevant evidence (the FT Reliability Engineering team build from scratch; the centralised cyber function build during the Interim VP period; the Architect-Content phase where Luke ran architecture for a publishing platform).

For JDs with autonomy-killer signals: flag to Luke before drafting. Often the right move is to skip the application — the role's autonomy structure isn't fixable through the cover letter.

For ambiguous JDs: draft with autonomy as a stated preference in Luke's voice ("I'm at my best when given the technical autonomy to make calls in my domain") so the screen self-selects the right next conversation.

## How this changes interview prep

The first substantive question Luke should ask in any recruiter screen, after the standard openers:

> "Who currently makes the major technical decisions in this team / function, and how would that change with this hire?"

That single question reveals the autonomy structure in ~90 seconds and lets Luke make an informed yes/no decision before the process consumes more of his time. Cheap to ask, expensive to mis-judge without asking.

Related questions to follow up on if the answer is mixed:
- "Walk me through a recent technical decision in this domain — who proposed it, who signed it off, who could have over-ridden it?"
- "What would you expect a new senior hire in this role to be allowed to change in their first 90 days?"
- "If I disagree with [name above] on a technical call, what's the path to resolution?"

## What this does NOT change

Luke's stated openness to broadening the funnel (cyber-or-not, IC-or-EM, Staff-through-Director) is unaffected. The autonomy floor sits *underneath* all those preferences — it's the precondition, not the preference itself. A role that fails the autonomy test should be dropped regardless of how well it matches other criteria.
