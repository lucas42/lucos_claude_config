lucas42 pushes back on over-engineered first drafts in two specific, recurring ways: (1) prefers the simplest code shape that solves the actual problem over generalized/defensive scoping (e.g. content-based gating, extra abstraction layers) — "seems much too complicated" — even when the more defensive version has a real, articulable safety rationale behind it; (2) wants code comments kept to 1-2 lines explaining *what*/*why* at a high level — background, alternatives considered, and design-discussion narrative belongs in the commit message or the GitHub ticket, not inline in the code.

**Why:** Observed twice in direct succession on the same PR (lucas42/lucos_creds#477, 2026-07-20): first round rejected a `isPemArmored`-gated normalization (content-shape scoping added after a legitimate lucos-security concern) in favour of a plain unconditional one-liner; second round rejected that one-liner living in its own module with a 6-line rationale comment, in favour of inlining it at the call site with a single-line comment. Both times the simpler version turned out to be *correct*, not just simpler — e.g. round 2's real invariant (browser textarea submission is a one-way CRLF-mangling channel, so there's no "legitimate CRLF" case to protect) was actually tighter than the original defensive scoping, once someone worked through why the simplification held up rather than just being terser.

**How to apply:** When writing code for lucas42-reviewed repos (or by extension, any lucos repo where he's a stakeholder), default to the smallest, most direct implementation that satisfies the stated requirement — don't preemptively add scoping/gating/defensive checks for hypothetical future misuse unless the risk is concrete and immediate, and don't split a small piece of logic into its own file/module unless it's reused elsewhere or needs isolated unit testing that can't happen otherwise. Keep code comments to what's needed to understand the line(s) at a glance; put rationale, alternatives-considered, and review history in the commit message and/or the linked ticket instead. If a teammate (e.g. lucos-security) has flagged a concrete concern that argues for more defensive code, it's still right to implement their ask and get their sign-off — but expect lucas42 may ask to simplify anyway, and be ready to surface the specific concern to him (as happened here) rather than silently keep or silently drop the defensive version.
